## Permits & Regulatory Stuff to Know

### Demolition
- Need a demolition permit from Superior Township / Washtenaw County before tearing anything down
- Pre-1978 structure = required asbestos and lead paint survey before demo. If found (very likely), licensed hazmat abatement required before demolition can proceed. Adds $5–20K and weeks of delay
- Cannot just chainsaw the house down

### New Construction
- Building permit required for any new structure
- New septic design and permit if the existing system won't support new construction
- Site plan may be required depending on what you build

### Tree / Land Clearing
- **Wetland buffers are the big one** — Michigan EGLE regulates wetlands. The three ponds likely trigger wetland designation. There are setback/buffer zones (can range from 25 to several hundred feet) where you cannot clear, grade, or build without a permit or a DEQ review
- Clearing near the ponds without checking this first is a legal risk
- Some municipalities require tree removal permits for trees over a certain diameter, especially near water
- Practically: chainsaw work on the upland areas away from ponds is probably fine. Near the ponds, check first

### General
- Any significant grading or earthwork may need a grading permit
- Superior Township zoning office is the first call to make before planning anything serious

## Chainsaw / Excavator Reality

- You'd want to clear a large area for actual living/yard space — very doable on 6+ acres
- Start away from the ponds (no wetland permit complications)
- Chainsaw: realistic for felling. Stump grinding is a separate thing — rent a grinder or hire it out
- Excavator rental: ~$500–1,500/day depending on size. You can move a lot of dirt and pull stumps in a few days with a decent machine
- Poison ivy has to come first — you can't safely chainsaw through dense poison ivy. Treat it (herbicide or professional remediation) before clearing work
- The whole process is doable but it's a multi-month project before the land feels open
- **The steep terrain complicates clearing** — south and west portions of the property have serious grades (see topo survey). Chainsaw work on a slope with downed trees is harder and more dangerous. Excavator is more valuable here than on flat land.
- If you want to level any area for a pad or structure, you're looking at cut-and-fill grading work, not just clearing

## Terrain / Topography (from 1997 Survey)

- A topographic survey from March 1997 exists — done when the house was moved. The estate should have it.
- The land is genuinely rolling. Significant grade change across the property, steepest on the south and west sides.
- The house sits on a mid-parcel high point. The ponds sit in natural drainage depressions.
- **Usable flat area is much less than 6 acres.** The realistic build zones are: the plateau around the current house, and the 1-acre south parcel (garage parcel, which looks more open in aerial).
- The steep zones are beautiful but constrain where you can build without significant grading/retaining work.
- Any new construction on sloped terrain = grading permit + retaining walls potentially + engineered foundation
